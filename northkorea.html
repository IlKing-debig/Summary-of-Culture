North Korea


Overview

North Korea is a country in East Asia, occupying the northern half of the Korean Peninsula. It is bordered by China to the north, Russia to the northeast, and South Korea to the south, separated by the Korean Demilitarized Zone (DMZ).


History

  • Division: After World War II, the Korean Peninsula was divided along the 38th parallel, with the Soviet Union occupying the north and the United States occupying the south.

  • The Korean War (1950-1953): North Korea invaded the South, leading to a devastating war that ended in an armistice (a ceasefire), though no formal peace treaty was ever signed.

  • The Kim Dynasty: Since its founding in 1948, the country has been ruled by three generations of the Kim family: Kim Il Sung (the Eternal President), Kim Jong Il, and the current leader, Kim Jong Un.

Government and Ideology

  • Political System: It is a highly centralized, one-party state led by the Workers' Party of Korea.

  • Juche: The official state ideology is Juche, which emphasizes national self-reliance, independence, and a strong military.

  • Songun: A "military-first" policy that prioritizes the Korean People's Army in state affairs and resource allocation.

Economy

  • Command Economy: The state controls almost all aspects of the economy, including industry and agriculture.

  • Isolation: Due to its nuclear weapons program and human rights record, North Korea is subject to heavy international sanctions, which significantly limit its global trade.

  • Resources: Despite economic challenges, the country possesses significant mineral resources, including coal, iron ore, and rare earth metals.

Geography and Society

  • Landscape: The country is very mountainous, with the Kaechon and Hamgyong ranges covering much of the territory. The highest point is Mount Paektu, which is considered sacred in Korean culture.

  • Capital: Pyongyang is the largest city and serves as the political, industrial, and cultural center.

  • Human Rights: International organizations frequently report on severe restrictions on freedom of speech, movement, and religion, as well as the existence of political prison camps.

Military and Nuclear Program

  • Army Size: North Korea maintains one of the largest standing armies in the world.

  • Nuclear Weapons: The country has conducted several nuclear tests and developed long-range ballistic missiles, which remains a major point of tension in international relations.
