Bread

What is bread?

Bread is a staple food prepared from a dough of flour (usually wheat) and water, usually by baking. Throughout recorded history and around the world, it has been an important part of much of the human diet.


History

  • Prehistoric Roots: Bread-making dates back to the Neolithic era. The earliest breads were likely flat cakes made from crushed cereal grains mixed with water and cooked on hot stones.

  • Ancient Civilizations: The Egyptians are credited with discovering fermentation (leavening), which resulted in lighter, airier loaves. The Greeks and Romans further refined baking techniques, turning it into a commercial industry with public bakeries.

  • Industrialization: During the Industrial Revolution, the production of bread moved from the home to large factories, leading to the development of sliced bread and enriched flours.


Ingredients and preparation

  1. Flour: While wheat is the most common due to its high gluten content (which helps the bread rise), rye, barley, maize (corn), and oats are also frequently used.

  2. Leavening: Most bread is leavened by yeast, which ferments the sugars in the dough to produce carbon dioxide. Some breads use chemical leaveners (like baking soda) or remain unleavened (flatbreads).

  3. Process: The typical process involves mixing ingredients, kneading the dough to develop gluten, allowing it to rise (proofing), and finally baking.

Global Varieties

Bread takes many forms depending on the culture:


  • Crusty Loaves: Such as the French Baguette or Italian Ciabatta.

  • Flatbreads: Including Middle Eastern Pita, Indian Naan, and Mexican Tortillas.

  • Sourdough: Uses a "starter" of naturally occurring yeast and bacteria, giving it a distinct tangy flavor.

  • Quick Breads: Like muffins or biscuits that use chemical leavening instead of yeast.

Nutritional Value

Bread is a major source of carbohydrates (starch). Whole-grain breads are also significant sources of dietary fiber, B vitamins, and minerals. Many commercial breads are "enriched" with added nutrients to replace those lost during the milling process.


Cultural and Religious Significance

In many cultures, bread is a metaphor for basic necessities and living conditions (e.g., "earning a crust" or "breadwinner"). It also holds deep religious significance, such as the Eucharist in Christianity or Matzo in Judaism.
